02

Product & positioning

Sóró is two products in one: a panel of verified Nigerian university students (the respondent side) and a B2B research service that sells access to that panel via AI-moderated interviews (the company side).

The core insight

The UX is the data-quality argument. Bad survey UX (Google Forms) makes students rush and lie. A conversational, paid, engaging experience gets honest answers — which is the actual product brands are paying for, not "fun design."

What gets sold

Not raw data, not a dashboard — a research report backed by verified respondents: summary of findings, demographic breakdown, verbatim quotes, CSV + PDF export. Companies pay upfront before any survey is deployed, which removes Sóró's capital risk entirely.

Why university students first

  • Most brand-hungry, fastest-growing consumer segment in Nigeria
  • Verifiable in a way generic "Gen Z" panels aren't (university email / student ID)
  • About to enter peak earning years — brands want them before habits solidify
  • Verification directly solves the data-quality problem competitors have

Outreach templates

Connection request — the formula

One job: get accepted. Under 300 characters. One specific observation. No pitch, no ask.

Fintech templateSaw [Company]'s campus account push — interesting move. Building something for exactly that market. Would love to connect, [First Name].
International org templateSpotted your work on [Study/Programme]. Building something adjacent for Nigerian youth research. Would love to connect.
Generic fallbackBuilding a verified Nigerian university student research panel — saw your work in [space]. Would love to connect.

First message — after they accept (wait 2–3 days)

Under 5 lines. Pick ONE ask. Never request a call in message one.

Fintech exampleHey [Name] — [Company]'s student account is the right call. I imagine you're partly flying blind on how that cohort actually thinks about digital banking vs. agents though. We run AI-moderated interviews with verified students from UNILAG, UI, OAU etc. — structured reports in 48 hours. Is student research something your team runs at all?

Rules to memorize

  • Connection request stays under 300 characters — LinkedIn cuts it off
  • Never mention price, "platform," or "solution" in the connection request
  • Always end with their first name — converts better
  • If no specific hook exists, spend 2 minutes on their profile before sending
  • One ask per message — pick from: "Is this something your team runs?" / "Want a sample report?" / "Worth a look?"
  • No reply after 5 days → one follow-up, then let go

16 — Phase 3

The data API — the actual $100M unlock

"Every brief you run grows a proprietary dataset of African youth consumer behaviour. Searchable. Queryable. Sellable infinitely. One insight gets generated once and sold to a hundred buyers."

Every brief Sóró runs — every conversation, every opinion, every demographic data point — gets stored. By the time Phase 3 launches, Sóró will have hundreds of studies worth of proprietary African youth insights that nobody else on the continent has collected in this form. That dataset is the real product. The API is just how you sell access to it.

Why this changes the entire business

Brief model (Phase 1)

Time-constrained. Capacity-constrained. Earn once per brief. Revenue scales with your team's bandwidth.

Data API (Phase 3)

Near-zero marginal cost. Sell the same dataset to 500 buyers. Revenue scales with the size of the dataset, not your team.

Three product layers

1
Insight Search Portal
A company types a question — "what do Nigerian university students think about mobile savings accounts?" — and gets synthesised findings from existing Sóró data. Pay per search ($5–$50) or monthly subscription ($200–$500/mo). Closest comparison: Bloomberg Terminal for African youth consumer data.
2
Developer API
A fintech plugs Sóró's API into their product. Every time they need consumer context — sentiment on a feature, preference data for a cohort — they query and get structured JSON back. Priced on a credit model (prepaid, per query) or monthly tiers. Reference: Twilio charges per API call; OpenAI charges per token. Sóró charges per insight query.
3
Bulk Data Licensing
Large orgs (Unilever global, government agencies, impact funds, AI companies training models on African demographic data) pay annual flat fees for bulk anonymised dataset access. One deal = recurring revenue. Reference: Numerator licenses its consumer panel data to universities, hedge funds, and CPG giants for six-figure annual fees.

API pricing model — what works for data products

Data APIs don't follow the same pricing logic as service APIs. Usage is irregular — a company might query intensively before a product launch, then go quiet for months. Research shows the best models for this pattern are:

The context connector — Sóró as a RAG layer for AI platforms

This is distinct from selling training data. Training data improves a model permanently and statically. A context connector plugs into an existing AI platform and gives it live, queryable African consumer intelligence at decision time — without retraining anything.

How it works
1
A fintech's AI product manager bot is deciding what to recommend to a 21-year-old Lagos student
2
Instead of relying on generic training data, it queries the Sóró connector: "what do Lagos university students aged 19–22 think about savings products?"
3
Gets back structured, current, verified African consumer context. Makes a better decision.

This is essentially RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) as a service — but the retrieval corpus is Sóró's proprietary African consumer panel, not the open web. Every AI platform that wants to serve African markets well has this problem: their models were trained mostly on Western data and perform poorly when making decisions about African consumers. Sóró becomes the missing context layer.

Who plugs this in
  • Fintechs with AI recommendation engines (Kuda, Carbon, Cowrywise)
  • E-commerce platforms personalising for Nigerian users
  • AI copilots built for African businesses
  • Marketing AI tools targeting African Gen Z
  • Any company building on Claude, GPT, or Gemini and serving African markets
How it's sold

A Sóró MCP (Model Context Protocol) connector — the standard emerging for giving AI models access to external knowledge sources. Listed on Claude's MCP marketplace, OpenAI's plugin store, and LangChain's tool registry. Developers install it in minutes. Priced per query or monthly subscription. The flywheel: more AI platforms use it → more queries → more revenue → funds more research → better context → more platforms want it.

The compounding advantage: Unlike training data (sold once, static) and unlike custom briefs (sold once per client), the context connector generates revenue every time an AI makes a decision using Sóró's context. If a fintech's recommendation engine fires 10,000 times a day and queries Sóró each time, that's 10,000 billable events daily from one integration. The panel gets more valuable as it grows, and the connector revenue scales automatically with the AI platforms' own growth.

The AI training data angle — two distinct assets

This is the one nobody is talking about yet, and Sóró actually has two things AI companies will pay for, not one.

Asset 1 — Consumer insight data

AI companies building models for African markets — African language models, fintech AI, healthcare AI, recommendation engines — need high-quality, demographically rich African consumer data to train on. Structured JSON of what verified Nigerian students think, buy, prefer, and spend on is exactly that. Datarade already lists Numerator and similar panel providers as AI training data sources. Sóró could be the first African panel listed there.

Asset 2 — Conversational AI survey experience data

Every AI-moderated interview Sóró runs produces something rarer than insight data: natural, contextual, human conversation turns between an AI interviewer and a real African person. This is high-quality dialogue data — with demographic metadata attached (age, school, state, interests). AI companies training conversational models, voice assistants, customer service bots, or any dialogue system targeting African users will pay premium for this. It's not just what people think — it's how they talk. Nigerian Pidgin, code-switching between English and Yoruba/Igbo/Hausa, informal speech patterns, cultural references. That linguistic texture is nearly impossible to collect at scale any other way. Sóró collects it automatically as a byproduct of the core product.

When to build this

Not yet. The API is only worth building when the dataset has genuine depth — at minimum 100+ briefs across diverse topics and demographics. That's roughly a year of active brief-running at current pace. Store the data cleanly from day one (structured JSON, tagged by topic, category, demographic, university, date) so the API layer is easy to build on top when the time comes.

Scaling plays — what the data says

Deep research into African market trends surfaces eight legitimate expansion plays beyond the core Nigeria student panel. Ranked by how naturally they extend from what Sóró already is.

1. Expand demographics vertically — all verified young Africans

African household spending in Sub-Saharan Africa has grown 150% faster than the population over the past 20 years. Total household consumption is forecast to hit $2.5 trillion by 2030. Nigeria alone has ~52 million people in the consumer class. University students are the seed — but verified young professionals, recent graduates, and NYSC participants are the natural next ring. Same verification model, much larger addressable panel.

Tactical move: After 10k verified students on panel, open a "Young Professionals" tier — 22–30 years old, verified by employment + NIN. Brands pay a premium for post-graduate earners vs. students.

2. Pan-Africa expansion — Ghana, Kenya, South Africa first

Nigeria is 28% of all African fintechs and commands 26% of the continent's e-commerce revenue. But Kenya, Ghana, and South Africa each have their own fintech ecosystems (1,200+ fintechs active across Africa by mid-2024), FMCG giants, and NGO presence. The panel model works identically. Cost to expand: mainly student recruitment in new markets via local TikTok/content strategy. Same platform, different phone number verification and local payout rails (M-Pesa for Kenya, etc.).

3. International clients — the real revenue multiplier

Every multinational entering Africa needs consumer data and has no trusted African panel to buy from. Nigeria accounts for a disproportionate share of Africa's consumer class — Lagos and Johannesburg alone each have 8M+ consumer-class residents. The companies that want this data have USD budgets:

  • FMCG multinationals (Kraft Foods, Johnson & Johnson, Unilever global) ramping up Africa presence — pay $5k–$50k per custom research engagement
  • Venture capital and private equity conducting due diligence on African consumer businesses — one-off high-value data pulls
  • African Development Bank, World Bank, IFC — constantly funding youth employment and consumer studies, USD grants
  • Global management consultants (McKinsey, BCG) publishing African consumer reports — would pay for proprietary panel access as a primary source

4. Syndicated reports — sell once, earn many times

Instead of only custom briefs (one buyer per study), produce quarterly syndicated reports on specific topics — "State of Gen Z Finance in Nigeria Q1 2026", "Campus Consumer Trends Report", "Nigerian Youth and E-commerce 2026." These get produced once using your existing panel and sold to 20–50 buyers at ₦50k–₦200k each. Nigeria's FMCG sector is projected at $38 billion — every major player in that market is a potential syndicated report buyer.

Math: One syndicated report, produced for ₦500k in research costs, sold to 30 companies at ₦150k each = ₦4.5M revenue. 10× the economics of a single custom brief for the same underlying data.

5. Brand tracking subscriptions

Instead of one-off briefs, brands pay a monthly retainer to track their metrics on the panel continuously — brand awareness, NPS, product perception, category preference. This is Kantar's highest-margin product line. For Sóró: a fintech pays ₦200k/month to see their brand health score among students updated weekly. Locked-in recurring revenue, minimal incremental work once the tracking survey is templated.

6. Recruit-as-a-service for African companies

The verified panel of university students is also a talent pipeline. Nigerian companies hiring for entry-level roles, internships, and graduate programs spend significant money on campus recruitment. Sóró already has verified, engaged, academically-enrolled students. A "Recruit" product that lets companies post roles directly to relevant panel members is a zero-infrastructure revenue line — the panel does double duty as a job board. Charge ₦50–₦200k per placement or per campaign.

7. White-label panel for research agencies

Nigerian and pan-African research agencies (the ones currently running manual focus groups and expensive fieldwork) could license Sóró's panel and survey infrastructure under their own brand. They bring the client relationships and research expertise; Sóró provides the verified respondents and AI-moderated interview platform. Revenue share model — agency keeps 40%, Sóró keeps 60%. Instant distribution into the existing market research buyer ecosystem without building a sales team.

8. Government and policy research

African governments, state agencies, and electoral commissions need youth sentiment data — for policy design, public health campaigns, electoral research. This is a large, underserved, price-insensitive buyer category. INEC (Nigerian electoral commission), state ministries, NDDC — these institutions have budgets and no modern research infrastructure. One government contract could be worth ₦5–₦20M.

9. Behavioral intelligence — the social graph layer

This is the most premium data product on the roadmap, and the one no African research firm is anywhere close to building. The idea: with user consent, students optionally connect their Instagram and TikTok accounts to Sóró. What they follow, what they engage with, what content patterns define their digital behaviour — layered on top of their survey responses. Self-reported data (what surveys capture) combined with observed behavioural data (what social media reveals) is an entirely different category of consumer intelligence.

"A student says they prefer savings products. Their TikTok follows tell you they're obsessed with streetwear drops. That tension is exactly what a brand needs to know to reach them."
What gets captured
  • Instagram: accounts followed, content categories engaged with, brand interactions
  • TikTok: content categories, creator types, product discovery patterns, trending audio/format exposure
  • Cross-platform: what they say (surveys) vs. what they do (social) — the gap between these is where real insight lives
Who pays for this
  • Brand strategists building Gen Z campaigns — need to know what culture students actually live in
  • Fintech growth teams — why do students open TikTok but not their savings app?
  • Media buyers — what platforms, creators, and formats reach this cohort organically
  • VC and private equity — real behavioral signals when evaluating consumer-facing businesses
⚠️ Build this carefully

This is the most legally sensitive product on the roadmap. Requires explicit, granular opt-in consent — not buried in terms. Students must understand exactly what is shared, what is anonymised, and what they earn for it. Get a data privacy lawyer to review the consent flow before building. NDPR (Nigeria Data Protection Regulation) compliance is non-negotiable. When done right, this is a $10M+ product. When done wrong, it kills the core business. Phase 4 minimum — after the panel has significant trust and a legal foundation is in place.

10. Sell conversational data to AI companies

Every Sóró interview produces two things: consumer insights (what people think) and dialogue data (how people talk). The second is arguably worth more to a specific buyer category — AI labs and companies training conversational models for African markets.

The scarcity here is real. High-quality, naturally-occurring African conversational data — in Nigerian Pidgin, code-switched English/Yoruba/Igbo/Hausa, informal registers — barely exists in any training dataset. Most African AI training data is scraped web text, which is formal, biased toward English, and doesn't reflect how people actually speak. Sóró's interviews are:

  • Contextual — real questions about real topics, not synthetic prompts
  • Demographically tagged — age, university, state, gender attached to every conversation
  • Linguistically rich — captures code-switching, Pidgin, slang, informal registers that scraped data misses
  • Consented — students agree to data collection at sign-up, making it legally licenseable in ways scraped data isn't

Buyers: African AI labs (InstaDeep, Lelapa AI, Masakhane), voice assistant companies targeting Nigeria (OPay, PalmPay building in-app voice), international AI companies expanding to Africa (Google, Meta both have African language programs), and any company training customer service or chatbot models for Nigerian users. License structure: flat annual fee per language/demographic slice, or per-token pricing for the raw conversational corpus.

Critical implementation note: Build consent language into the student signup flow from day one — "your anonymised conversation data may be used to improve AI systems." Don't retrofit this later. Clean consent = licenseable asset. Missing consent = worthless data legally.
